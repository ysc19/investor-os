## ==P1-M3 **Revenue: The Lifeblood of Every Business**==

Revenue is the income earned from a company's primary business activities before any expenses are deducted.

When you see revenue, don't just write down the number.

Ask:

- Is revenue growing?
- Is growth organic or acquisition-driven?
- Is it recurring?
- Which business segment is growing fastest?
- Which segment is slowing?
- How does management explain the changes?
- How does it compare with competitors?

Ask this question if you see growth, growth in comparision to what? the maket share or the prev year revenue?

There are **eight major sources of evidence**.

---

## 📄 1. Financial Statements (The Hard Evidence)

This is our current phase.

You examine:

- Revenue
- Expenses
- Profit
- Cash
- Debt
- Assets
- Liabilities

Example:

The CEO says:

> "Business is booming."

But you notice:

- Revenue down 8%
- Cash flow down 20%
- Debt up 40%

The numbers don't support the claim.

That's evidence.

---

## 📑 2. Annual Reports (10-K)

This is where management explains:

- What happened.
- Why it happened.
- Risks.
- Competition.
- Future strategy.

You don't just read the CEO's letter.

You compare what management says with what the financial statements show.

If they match, confidence increases.

If they don't, you investigate further.

---

## 🎙️ 3. Earnings Calls

Every quarter, management answers questions from analysts.

Analysts ask things like:

> "Why did gross margins fall?"

> "Why are customers leaving?"

> "Why is inventory increasing?"

These answers often reveal concerns or opportunities before they show up fully in the numbers.

---

## 📊 4. Industry Comparisons

Numbers don't exist in isolation.

Imagine:

Company A grows revenue by **10%**.

Sounds good.

But if every competitor grew **30%**, then Company A may actually be falling behind.

Always compare with peers.

---

## 👥 5. Customers

Ask:

- Why do people buy this product?
- Are customers loyal?
- Would they switch to a competitor if prices increased?

Strong customer demand often supports long-term success.

---

## 🏆 6. Competitors

If JPMorgan says:

> "We're gaining market share."

Check whether competitors are saying the opposite.

If everyone claims to be winning, someone is probably exaggerating.

Cross-checking is a powerful habit.

---

## 🌍 7. The Bigger Picture (Macro)

Sometimes a company struggles because of factors outside its control.

Examples:

- Interest rates rise.
- Oil prices spike.
- New regulations.
- A recession.
- Currency movements.

Good analysts separate company-specific problems from broader economic forces.

---

## 👨‍💼 8. Management's Actions

Don't just listen to what executives say.

Watch what they **do**.

Examples:

- Are they buying back shares?
- Paying dividends?
- Taking on excessive debt?
- Selling large amounts of their own stock?
- Making disciplined acquisitions?

Actions often reveal priorities more clearly than speeches.



# How Professionals Actually Read a 300-Page Annual Report

Imagine it as a city.

You don't visit every building on your first day.

You learn the important landmarks first.

Here's the order many analysts use on a first pass:

### 📍1. Business Overview (5–10 minutes)

Answer:

- What does this company do?
- How does it make money?
- What are its main business segments?

---

### 📍2. CEO Letter (10–15 minutes)

Read it skeptically.

You're looking for:

- What management says went well.
- Their strategy.
- Their explanation for results.

Later, you'll verify whether the numbers support the story.

---

### 📍3. Financial Statements (30–60 minutes)

This is where we'll spend most of our time.

- Income Statement
- Balance Sheet
- Cash Flow Statement

These are the backbone of the report.

---

### 📍4. Notes to the Financial Statements

These explain important details such as:

- Debt
- Revenue recognition
- Acquisitions
- Lawsuits
- Accounting policies

Think of them as the "fine print" that can change how you interpret the numbers.

---

### 📍5. Risk Factors

Every company must discuss major risks.

Examples:

- Competition
- Cybersecurity
- Interest rates
- Regulation
- Supply chain issues

Great analysts pay attention here because risks can change future performance.

---

### 📍6. Segment Information

This tells you which parts of the business are growing or shrinking.

For JPMorgan, you'll see different businesses like consumer banking, investment banking, commercial banking, and asset management.

---

# The 80/20 Rule

This is one of the biggest secrets in finance.

About **20% of the report contains most of the information you'll revisit regularly**.

The rest is still important, but you don't need to master it all at once.

Over time, you'll naturally become comfortable exploring more of it.
